Positive characteristics of dissertations

Introduction

This list is a compilation of characteristics originally proposed by third year undergraduate students as positive aspects of dissertations in 1999. In subsequent years I have added points raised by both undergraduates and postgraduates in workshops. I have organised the characteristics into groups and done minor editing to clarify and avoid too much duplication. In my view, most of these characteristics are required of all dissertations (e.g. well defined and clearly stated objectives), whereas some may be positive features of the specific example (e.g. timeline, tabulation of test results) and serve as suggestions.

Mary Dyson
  • Definition of scope
  • Well defined and clearly stated objectives might include what will not be covered for either practical reasons or need to limit scope
  • Restatement of aims/objectives in abstract, introduction and conclusion
  • Answering the question
  • Explanation of title making sure title is an accurate reflection of content of dissertation
  • Choice of terminology explained definition of terms
  • Good, detailed investigative research
  • Introduction of a theory to prove or disprove
  • Evidence to back up well-constructed arguments
  • Clear contextualization which is selective (identifying relevant context)
  • Inclusion of personal/own views as well as citing literature
  • Inclusion of interviews
  • Substantial conclusion
  • Description of research methods
  • Clarity of ideas (apparent through language and content)
  • Make abstract the essence of dissertation
  • A precise, concise and clear summary of the work
  • Introduction sets the scene
  • Possibly a combination of theory and history
  • Inclusion of primary data
  • Obvious enthusiasm for the subject
  • Originality
  • Creation of original archive
  • Descriptive and cautious
